# Homeward: A Short Story About the Homes We Leave Behind

The apartment was almost empty.

Three suitcases by the door. A stack of flattened cardboard boxes in the hallway. The kitchen counter, bare except for a roll of packing tape and a half-used roll of paper towels that wouldn't survive the journey.

Li Wei stood in the doorway of his room, running his fingers along the wall. The paint was the same off-white it had been when he moved in four years ago. Slightly yellowed near the ceiling where the humidifier worked hardest during winter.

He pressed his palm flat against the surface. Four years of warmth, absorbed into plaster.

## The Kitchen

The kitchen was where he had become Chinese again.

Not the Chinese of his passport or his parents' expectations, but a quieter, more private Chinese that lived in muscle memory and muscle memory alone.

His grandmother's recipe for 番茄炒蛋 — tomato and egg stir-fry — required no measuring cups. A dash of this, a generous pour of that, the right amount of oil determined by the sound it made in the wok. The sizzle that told you the temperature without a thermometer.

He had learned to cook in this kitchen because the restaurant near his university was too expensive and the cafeteria food had no soul.

Now, standing in the bare kitchen, he realized: the recipes weren't in his phone. They were in his hands.

## The Window

Window number three, facing east. Not the largest window in the building, but the one that caught the morning light in a particular way between March and April, when the sun was still low enough to come in sideways and paint a rectangle of gold on the floor.

He used to set his alarm for 6:47 — not 6:45, not 6:50, but 6:47, because that was when the light first reached his desk. Four minutes of perfect reading light before the overhead light became necessary.

Nobody had taught him this. The building hadn't been designed for this. But he had discovered it by accident one morning, and for three springs, he had protected that 6:47 ritual like a small, private religion.

Next year, in the new city, his window would face a different direction. The sun would come at a different angle. He would have to learn the light all over again.

## The Neighbors

He had never learned their names.

Mrs. Chen in 1402, who watered the hallway plants and never complained about his cooking smells. The young couple in 1405 whose baby cried between 2 and 3 AM on Tuesdays — not every Tuesday, but reliably enough that he had set mental alarms. Mr. Zhang in 1408, who left his door open to air out his apartment at exactly 5 PM every evening, as if the building itself had lungs that needed breathing.

They were strangers. They would remain strangers. But they were strangers who had shaped his schedule, his sleeping patterns, his tolerance for noise.

In the new building, there would be new strangers. New sounds. New tolerances to build.

The thought was exhausting. The thought was also, somehow, exciting.

## The Last Thing

He picked up the last thing: a photograph, slightly faded, of his family standing outside the old house in Hangzhou. His grandfather was not in the photo because he was the one holding the camera, a detail Li Wei only understood now that he, too, was learning to frame moments rather than simply be in them.

The photo was taken in 2009. Everyone was still alive. Everyone was still in China. The world was smaller and warmer and more certain than it would later become.

He thought about putting it in the suitcase. Then he thought about putting it in his pocket, close to his body, the way you carry something precious on a train.

He put it in his pocket.

## The Walk

The walk to the moving truck took four minutes. Four minutes and twelve seconds, according to his watch, though he hadn't timed it on purpose. The route was automatic: left out the door, past the plant Mrs. Chen watered, down two flights of stairs, through the lobby where the security guard nodded at him the way he nodded at everyone, across the small courtyard with the bench he had never sat on, and out to the street.

The driver was waiting. The suitcases went in. The boxes went in.

Li Wei looked up at the building. His window was dark. The rectangle of morning light would come tomorrow, for someone else.

He got in the car.

He did not look back for very long.

*There is a Chinese saying: 故土难离 — it is hard to leave the land where you grew up.*

*There is a counter-saying, unspoken, that everyone discovers eventually: the land doesn't leave you. You carry it in your hands, in your windows, in the names you never learned. You carry it homeward, wherever home comes to be.*
